Power Rankings vs. Tournament Results: A Legacy Gap Analysis

Two independent signals — card power scores and tournament prevalence — rarely agree. The divergence is the finding.

MTG Legacy data

There are two ways to ask how good a Magic card is. The first is analytical: read the text, evaluate the effect, assign a score. The second is empirical: count how often it shows up in the decks that actually win tournaments. These two signals are not the same thing, and the gap between them is more interesting than either one alone.

The correlation between power score and Legacy prevalence in this dataset is r = 0.137. That is not a typo. Knowing a card's power ranking tells you almost nothing about whether it will show up in a competitive Legacy deck. Force of Will is ranked #26,269 on the Modern power list and appears in 55% of Legacy decks. Brainstorm is unranked entirely and defines the format.

The ranking system was built on Modern. Legacy has different rules — different mana, different threats, different answers. It should surprise no one that the scores don't transfer.

The alternative-cost gap

Of the 330 mainboard cards that the power ranking system left unscored, roughly 160 are lands (CMC = 0, excluded by design), 59 are zero-or-X-cost spells like Chalice of the Void and Chrome Mox, and 111 are a more interesting category: cards whose effects are expressed in very short oracle text but whose impact on board state is enormous.

Blood Moon. Trinisphere. Reanimate. Each has fewer than twelve words in its rules text and each warps a game around its axis. A system trained to count keywords and abilities per mana spent doesn't know what to do with them. That's not a flaw in the data — it's a signal about what makes Legacy, Legacy.

Methodology

Tournament data was scraped from MTGTop8 covering the Legacy metagame (meta ID 64) from 2023–2025, yielding 1,847 deck lists across 312 events. Prevalence was computed under four weighting schemes: flat count, placement weight (9 − rank), prestige weight (event star rating), and a combined placement × prestige scheme. Top-5 cards were identical across all four.
